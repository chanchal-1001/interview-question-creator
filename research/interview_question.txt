Question: 1. What is correlation in statistics and how is it measured?Answer : Correlation in statistics is a measure that expresses the extent to which two variables are linearly related. It is used to identify relationships between variables without making a statement about cause and effect. Correlation coefficients range from -1 to 1, with 0 indicating no linear relationship, 1 indicating a perfect positive linear relationship, and -1 indicating a perfect negative linear relationship.-----------------------------------------------------------------Question: 1. Explain the range of correlation coefficients and what each value indicates.Answer : The range of correlation coefficients is from -1 to 1. Here is what each value indicates:

- A correlation coefficient of 0 indicates no linear relationship between the two variables.
- A correlation coefficient of 1 indicates a perfect positive linear relationship, meaning that as one variable increases, the other variable also increases proportionally.
- A correlation coefficient of -1 indicates a perfect negative linear relationship, meaning that as one variable increases, the other variable decreases proportionally.

These values help to understand the strength and direction of the relationship between the variables being studied.-----------------------------------------------------------------Question: 3. Why is it important to note that correlation does not equal causation?Answer : It is important to note that correlation does not equal causation because just because two variables are correlated, it does not necessarily mean that one variable causes the other. There could be other factors or variables at play that are influencing the relationship between the two variables. Making assumptions about causation based solely on correlation can lead to incorrect conclusions and decisions.-----------------------------------------------------------------Question: 9. Explain the example given about ice cream sales and shark attacks to illustrate the concept of correlation not implying causation.Answer : The example of ice cream sales and shark attacks is often used to highlight the concept that correlation does not imply causation. In this case, there is a strong correlation between the two variables, meaning that as ice cream sales increase, so do shark attacks. However, it would be incorrect to conclude that eating ice cream causes shark attacks or vice versa.

The reason for this correlation is likely due to a third variable, such as hot weather. During hot weather, people are more likely to buy ice cream and also more likely to go swimming in the ocean, increasing the chances of a shark attack. Therefore, the correlation between ice cream sales and shark attacks is not because one causes the other, but rather because they are both influenced by hot weather.

This example serves as a reminder that correlation is a statistical relationship and does not necessarily imply a direct cause-and-effect relationship between variables. It is essential to consider other factors and conduct further research to determine causation accurately.-----------------------------------------------------------------Question: 10.
